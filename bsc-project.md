## 📄 Abstract

> Since the emergence of nanotechnology as a field of science, it has been widely used by clothing, glass-making, automobile, paint, and medical industries. New studies have shown Quantum Dots (QD), a luminescent nanomaterial, with an immense use in bio-imaging, biosensors, photocatalysis, and optronics.
>
> The present research work aims at demonstration of scalable, rapid, and economically viable green synthesis of Carbon-based QDs (CQDs) using microwave pyrolysis method from Ginger Honey (GH) along with its characterization and biocompatibility studies on in vitro models such as micro-organisms, plants, cancerous and non-cancerous animal cell line.
>
> The synthesized CQDs were subjected to characterization for its absorbance, shape, size and analysis of presence of functional groups. GH derived CQDs displayed peak at 223nm, 283nm and 851nm during UV-Vis spectroscopy. Study of fluorescence emission spectrum excited at 350nm show maximum Photo-Luminescence (PL) at 325nm. FTIR and TEM investigation of CQDs reveals various surface function groups such as alkanes, carboxylic acid, ether and ester, and average size of 8-12nm respectively.
>
> On investigating the effect of synthesized and characterized CQDs on HT1080 and WRL-68 cell line, revealed non-significant change in the percent viability of the cells when compared to the control. Also, the current research work projects the non-significant antimicrobial activity of CQDs on E.Coli and S. aureus. Similar data of non-significant change was perceived when seeds of V. radiate and T. aestivum were exposed to CQDs.
>
> All these information obtained on biocompatibility open new area for further studies such as bioimaging, drug delivery, and biosensors, photocatalysis, LEDs, organic solar cells, supercapacitor, etc.
